In my time in ENC2135, I learned a different approach to writing. I'm a STEM
major, which almost unilaterally means that I have a difficult relationship with
writing assignments, to say the least. I'm a chronic procrastinator, and I truly
pity everyone who has to peer-review my work and I greatly appreciate their
patience. While time management and raw writing speed are still areas I need to
work on, I feel very comfortable thinking critically about the way audiences
will receive and digest the information that I'm writing about, at least
compared to the beginning of the semester.

For most of my writing career, my compositions were all written in a casual,
conversational voice, quite like how I am writing this reflection. Approaching
writing as if it were a conversation remains my biggest asset for making writing
assignments less daunting. This semester especially, however, I learned the
limits of that tool. A conversational voice is ill-suited for, say, an academic
piece, like what I wrote for Project 1. Admittedly, abandoning my casual tone
was a little bit outside my comfort zone. Outlining the structure of the piece
helped, but the speech still feels quite unnatural. To write in tht tone
effectively, you need to be able to take yourself seriously, and I suppose I'm
still getting used to viewing myself as a professional. I have to get over that
rut at some point, though, and that was one of the many areas I've grown while
in this course.

It can be especially difficult for me to consistently focus on activities that
don't necessarily pique my interest, but the creative freedom that this course
allowed did much to address that concern. Gone are the days of writing about
books that I would never read outside of class; I academically wrote about
_Minecraft_. I created a _YouTube Short_, complete with the AI slop voiceover. I
designed an _Instagram post_. Even the website that you are reading this on, I
designed from scratch using [Astro]. This course is among the last gen-ed
classes that I'll take in my academic career, and the entire time I was engaging
with topics that directly relate to my interests and field of study. In the
future, I can only see myself voluntarily taking another writing course if I
know for sure that it allows me the same creative freedom that this one did.
It's a critical part of my motivation.

I'm pleased with the quality of the work I've been able to produce during my
time in this course, even if my time management skills... leave a lot to be
desired, to say the least. I leave you with the fruits of my labor: below you
will find my composition for Project 1, which is all about the technical
challenges of creating a voxel engine with Minecraft-like features, like physics
simulations and advanced rendering. Below that, you'll find two of my artifacts
from Project 2, which is about the ethics of unpaid open-source contributions.

I hope that you find them as engaging to read as I found them to write. That's
all from me.
