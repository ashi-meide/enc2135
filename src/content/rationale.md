I’m trying to shape how my audience views open-source software. Specifically, I
want them to view open-source software as a public good and contributing as a
public service. Policymaking is outside the scope of this project, so I’m mainly
targeting the public and their perspective on the importance of open source
software. A person’s opinion of something is always shaped by their prior
understanding of that subject. Because of this, I’ll be structuring my artifacts
around specific groups based on their knowledge of open-source projects. For a
lay audience, the primary challenge is not disagreement but lack of awareness.
Many people use open-source software daily without thinking about the
institutions that provide them with that service. In response, my artifacts
aimed at this group focus on introducing the concept of community-driven
software by referencing widely used projects and systems, and by illustrating
how these systems support everyday technology. Rather than overwhelming the
audience with technical detail, I focus on building a conceptual bridge between
familiar technologies and the invisible infrastructure that supports them. From
there, I guide the audience toward considering whether such infrastructure is
essential enough to warrant public funding.

Social media sites like Instagram, Threads, X, and Reddit allow users to submit
an ordered array of photos for followers to view. These posts are easily
reposted and shared by users that wish to contribute to the conversation I’m
trying to create. Because of the way photos are viewed on these platforms, their
linear scrolling nature (often referred to as a “carousel”) can be exploited to
create engaging or even somewhat interactive experiences for the user viewing
the post, which will help it stick out in their memory. This first artifact, a
social media carousel, will primarily target the layperson, being that the
majority of social media users, and people in general, are not programmers who
would be familiar with these topics. The micro-interactivity of the carousel’s
images are thus critical to helping this audience remain engaged with the topic,
since they might otherwise be disinterested.

The second artifact will be a short-form video for video-based services on
social media sites like TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts. This
artifact will also target the layperson. Short-form video content is
characterized by extremely limited attention spans and high competition for
viewer engagement; as a result, the artifact must capture attention immediately
and maintain it through both visual and auditory stimuli. Conventional content
in this form will pair the main message—in our case, that open-source software
is a public good—with a low-distraction background. The background footage need
not necessarily even be relevant to the discussion at hand; its role is to
captivate the viewer long enough to become interested in the message being
presented. I used footage from a parkour-style video game with subdued tones and
consistent motion.

The third artifact, a technical blog post hosted on a platform such as Medium,
targets a more specialized audience of computer science students and
early-career developers. Unlike the general audience, this group already
possesses the foundational knowledge necessary to understand open-source
software. Therefore, the rhetorical goal shifts from awareness to reframing and
persuasion. In this artifact, I adopt a more analytical tone and expand on the
systemic implications of underfunded open-source infrastructure. The blog format
is appropriate for this because it allows for sustained, linear argumentation
and the inclusion of more detailed explanations, examples, and reasoning. I
focus on explaining how modern software ecosystems depend on layers of
open-source dependencies, many of which are maintained by a very small number of
contributors. This connects to broader economic and structural issues, primarily
the imbalance between corporate reliance on open-source software and the lack of
financial support for its maintainers. This allows me to argue that public
funding is not just ethically justified, but practically necessary for long-term
security and reliability.

The differences between these artifacts reflect the differing needs and
expectations of their audiences. The first two artifacts must overcome initial
disinterest and limited attention, requiring strong visual design and simplified
messaging. In contrast, the blog post assumes a baseline level of interest and
technical literacy, allowing for a more in-depth and logically structured
argument. Each artifact therefore emphasizes different rhetorical
strategies—accessibility and engagement for the general audience, and depth and
analytical rigor for the technical audience—while maintaining a consistent
central message. If I were to extend this project further, I would explore
incorporating more explicit policy-oriented content, particularly in the blog
post or in an additional policy brief artifact. While this project focuses on
shaping perception, the next logical step would be translating that perception
into actionable proposals. Additionally, one challenge I encountered was
balancing accuracy with accessibility, particularly when simplifying complex
technical systems for a general audience. Ensuring clarity without
oversimplifying the issue required careful consideration of what details were
strictly necessary to include and which were not.
